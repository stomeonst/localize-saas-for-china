# Evidence and Channel Rules

Read this reference before drafting customer-facing claims or selecting channels.

## Evidence hierarchy

Prefer sources in this order:

1. Product documentation, official security pages, legal pages, status pages, and the company's own current website.
2. Government, regulator, standards-body, or official platform documentation.
3. Direct statements from named executives in an official announcement.
4. Reputable reporting that clearly identifies its source and publication date.
5. User-provided facts, labelled as user provided until independently verified.

Do not use scraped directories or anonymous posts as proof for customer-facing claims.

## Claim classes

### Verified

A current, relevant source directly supports the claim. Record its URL and the date checked.

An approved product facts sheet may count as verified when it identifies an accountable product owner, includes a current date, and clearly states that the facts are approved for external use. Record that provenance.

### User provided and pending verification

The user supplied the fact without a current primary source or accountable product-owner approval. It may guide internal hypotheses. Treat it as missing for customer-facing copy until verified.

### Inferred

The claim is a plausible interpretation of evidence. Keep it in internal rationale and label it as an inference. Do not move it into published copy without approval.

### Missing

No adequate support was found. Convert it into a question, evidence request, or launch blocker.

## Default launch gates

Unless verified evidence already exists, place all three in the launch-blocker section:

1. Mainland network accessibility for the product, authentication, essential integrations, and the selected CTA path.
2. Data flow, processing location, retention, subprocessors, security controls, and buyer-facing contractual documentation.
3. Contract entity, payment route, tax documentation, invoicing capability, and procurement ownership for the intended buyer.

## High-risk claims

Require direct evidence before using any statement about:

1. Chinese regulatory compliance.
2. Data residency or local hosting.
3. Cybersecurity certification.
4. Availability inside mainland China.
5. Chinese invoicing, payment, tax, or contract support.
6. Chinese-language customer support.
7. Named customers or partners.
8. Performance, accuracy, savings, or return on investment.
9. Market leadership or category rank.
10. Guaranteed outcomes.

When evidence is absent, write a neutral alternative. For example, replace a claim of full local compliance with a statement that deployment and data requirements should be reviewed for the buyer's use case.

## Brand naming

Keep the existing Latin brand by default. If the user requests a Chinese name:

1. Generate three candidates using different strategies, such as phonetic, semantic, and hybrid naming.
2. Explain pronunciation, literal meaning, intended association, and possible ambiguity.
3. Label all candidates exploratory.
4. Recommend trademark, domain, platform-handle, and native-speaker checks before adoption.

## Channel selection

### Chinese landing page

Use for every viable market-entry pack. Lead with the buyer's operational problem, the product mechanism, credible proof, and one feasible CTA.

### WeChat article

Use for B2B education, partner enablement, professional thought leadership, and launch explanation. Write for scanning, with a concrete title, short opening, useful sections, proof, and a restrained CTA.

### Xiaohongshu

Use when an individual user, creator, founder, practitioner, or small business owner can personally experience and discuss the product. Avoid it for a narrow enterprise procurement product unless the user has a credible practitioner angle.

### Bilingual sales email

Use for founder-led sales, partner outreach, and early discovery. Keep the first email concise, specific to the recipient, and focused on one problem and one next step. Do not claim personal research that was not performed.

If no verified recipient-specific signal and official source are available, retain a visible placeholder such as `[verified company signal, official source, date checked]`. Never fabricate a plausible personalization line.

### Developer community content

Use when the product has a working technical path, clear documentation, and verifiable integration details. Prefer a reproducible example over marketing superlatives.

## Localization quality

1. Translate the buying logic before translating sentences.
2. Use concrete nouns and verbs.
3. Reduce empty claims such as intelligent, leading, empowering, and one-stop unless evidence or category convention justifies them.
4. Preserve necessary technical terms in English on first use when Chinese terminology is ambiguous.
5. Back translate headlines and CTAs so an English-speaking stakeholder can review the intended meaning.
6. Keep tone credible. Avoid exaggerated urgency, fake scarcity, and fabricated social proof.

## Final reverse scan

Before release, search the whole customer-facing draft for statements about:

1. Chinese-language support.
2. Compliance or regulatory approval.
3. Customers, partners, or market presence.
4. Performance, savings, accuracy, or return on investment.
5. Payment, contracts, tax documents, or invoicing.
6. Data location, encryption, certifications, or security controls.
7. Mainland network availability.

For each result, link it to direct evidence in the ledger. If direct evidence is absent, remove it from public copy and place it in internal notes as a question or blocker.
